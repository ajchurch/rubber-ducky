## 1) Apple 'Darksword' Hack

The DarkSword hack is a mobile exploit chain that targeting Apple's iPhones and iPads running older software, specifically versions iOS 18.4 through 18.7. It is a "zero-click" or "one-click" attack, meaning a device can be compromised simply by visiting a malicious or hacked website through Safari (no file downloads or app installations are required). Once triggered, the exploit uses a series of six vulnerabilities to bypass Apple's security layers, allowing attackers to steal messages, passwords, location data, and even cryptocurrency wallet credentials in a matter of seconds.

What makes DarkSword even worse is that its code was recently leaked on GitHub, making it accessible to low-level cybercriminals rather than just elite state-sponsored groups. Unlike traditional spyware that remains on a phone for long-term monitoring, DarkSword uses a "hit-and-run" approach, grabbing the desired data immediately and then deletes its own files to leave no forensic trace.

## 2) Handala

The Handala Hack Team is a pro-Palestinian, Iran-linked cyber threat group that emerged in late 2023 following the outbreak of the Israel-Hamas war. The group is named after the Palestinian cartoon character **Handala**, a symbol of resistance. 

The group primarily uses 'hack-and-leak' operations and destructive 'wiper' malware, which is designed to permanently delete data and disrupt critical infrastructure rather than seeking a ransom. 

Recently, the group significantly escalated its activities towards the United States, most notably through a massive cyberattack on the medical technology giant [Stryker Corporation](https://www.stryker.com/au/en/index.html). This attack reportedly disrupted global operations and impacted systems used by millions of patients, framed by the group as retaliation for US military actions.

Further, Handala has claimed high-profile breaches of government officials, including the personal email of FBI Director Kash Patel and the devices of several senior Israeli politicians. Their tactics often involve exploiting weak 'cyber hygiene' and using social engineering, such as distributing malware disguised as legitimate IT fixes or software updates.

## 3) North Korea Axios Cyberattack

On March 31st 2026, North Korean hackers performed a highly sophisticated 'supply chain' cyberattack targeting Axios. Rather than attacking a specific company, hackers from the North Korean threat group **UNC1069** hijacked the NPM (Node Package Manager) account of a lead maintainer for the Axios library (a popular JavaScript tool used by developers to make HTTP requests). Once in control, they published two malicious versions of the library (versions 1.14.1 and 0.30.4) that included a hidden backdoor called WAVESHAPER.V2.

Axios is downloaded over 100 million times per week and is embedded in roughly 80% of cloud environments, so the potential impact was massive. The malware was designed to be cross-platform, affecting Windows, macOS, and Linux systems by granting the attackers remote shell access and the ability to grab sensitive data. To evade detection, the malicious code was programmed to delete itself and replace the library with a clean version after the initial infection.

Sources: https://cloud.google.com/blog/topics/threat-intelligence/north-korea-threat-actor-targets-axios-npm-package/

## 4) Crunchyroll Data Breach

In March 2026, Crunchyroll had a data breach where around 100 GB of data was stolen from its customer support systems. A hacker compromised the account of a support agent at **Telus International**, a third-party vendor used by Crunchyroll. By infecting the agent's workstation with malware to steal login credentials, the attacker gained roughly 24 hours of access to internal platforms, including Zendesk, Slack, and Google Workspace (very important platforms used primarily for communications and responding to tickets). The compromised information includes full names, usernames, email addresses, IP addresses, and general geographic locations which affected approximately 6.8 million Crunchyroll users.

Sources: https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/crunchyroll-probes-breach-after-hacker-claims-to-steal-68m-users-data/

## 5) Match Group Data Breach

In January 2026, the Match Group (parent company to dating apps such as Tinder, Hinge, and Match.com) had a data breach by the threat actor group **ShinyHunters** which claimed to had stolen over 10 million user records. The breach was executed through a "vishing" (voice phishing) campaign, targeting an employee’s single sign-on credentials, which allowed the attackers to access internal documents and data from the mobile analytics platform AppsFlyer. The exposed data included user IDs, IP addresses, and subscription transaction details however, Match Group stated that there was no evidence that sensitive information such as account passwords, financial details, or private chat histories were compromised.
